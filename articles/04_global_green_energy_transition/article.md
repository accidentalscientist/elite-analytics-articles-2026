Global renewable rankings are useful and incomplete. Large economies lead in absolute output; smaller economies can lead by share. Neither metric alone reveals whether a country is building a durable transition.

## Look for trajectories, not podiums

This analysis follows renewable output through time and uses clustering to group countries with similar growth patterns. That changes the question from “who is first?” to “which transition pathways are emerging?” Countries can share a trajectory even when their current scale is very different.

## Why the comparison matters

An established renewable leader, an economy growing rapidly from a small base and a country with a large fossil legacy face different challenges. Treating them as one contest produces poor policy lessons. The cluster view helps identify peers with comparable conditions, technologies or rates of change.

## The policy question

The useful next step is to ask which policies and infrastructure choices make a trajectory credible: transmission, finance, industrial capacity, market design or resource quality. Data cannot answer that alone, but it gives decision makers a better starting point than a top-ten chart.
