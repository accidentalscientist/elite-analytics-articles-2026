## National averages hide local realities

Countries are often compared through a single inequality statistic. That can be useful, but it obscures the fact that opportunity is experienced in cities, suburbs and regions. A national trend may look stable while the distribution of income within major urban areas changes sharply.

This analysis focuses on metropolitan income inequality through time, using Gini-coefficient data to examine coverage, consistency and variation across cities. Before drawing conclusions, it asks a necessary methodological question: where is the data strong enough to support comparison?

## Coverage is part of the finding

Data gaps are not merely inconvenient. They shape which places can be monitored, compared and included in policy discussions. Identifying cities with complete temporal coverage distinguishes robust long-run evidence from isolated observations.

Once that foundation is clear, the trends become more meaningful. Some countries show declining inequality; others show widening gaps or substantial variation between their cities. The lesson is not that one number explains every urban economy. It is that city-level monitoring adds intelligence that national summaries cannot provide.

## Why inequality belongs in a systems portfolio

Energy, housing, transport and employment policy all land in unequal places. A transition that lowers emissions but raises costs for already constrained households will not be durable. Understanding inequality is therefore not a detour from systems analysis. It is part of designing systems people can live with.
