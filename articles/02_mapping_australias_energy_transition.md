## National targets meet local infrastructure

It is tempting to talk about Australia's energy transition as if the country were one machine. Maps make the limitation of that view obvious. Power stations are not evenly distributed: they cluster along population and industrial corridors, particularly on the east coast, and their fuel types reflect the infrastructure, resources and investment choices of each region.

That geography matters. A renewable target can be national, but its delivery requires local land, local grid capacity, regional workforce and connections between resource zones and demand centres. The map of existing stations is therefore more than a visual inventory. It is a record of the system the transition must work with.

## The mix is changing, but not everywhere at the same pace

The national energy series shows the familiar broad pattern: non-renewable generation remains substantial, while wind and solar have expanded rapidly from a small base. The interesting question is what that means state by state.

Some regions inherit large thermal fleets. Others have stronger hydro, wind or solar resources. These differences shape the practical choices available to planners. A place with retiring coal capacity faces a different sequencing challenge from a place with abundant renewable resources but limited transmission. Neither situation is solved by a single national average.

## A better way to read the map

The useful question is not which colour dominates a postcode or state. It is what the map suggests about the next constraint. Where are renewable resources strongest? Where is the grid already congested? Which communities are exposed to changes in legacy generation? Where could a new connection unlock both lower emissions and more resilient supply?

The transition will succeed through a series of regional decisions that add up to a national system. Maps help make those decisions visible.
