Rising seas make climate risk visible. Emissions data explains the systems behind that risk. Country and sector profiles show why the transition cannot be delivered through one uniform policy.

## Consequence, cause and choice

The sea-level record is a measurable consequence of a warming climate. Sectoral emissions identify the areas where change matters most. Country-level trends and clustering reveal that national systems differ: power, transport, industry and household emissions are not distributed in the same way everywhere.

## Better comparison, better cooperation

Totals matter, but they are not enough. Countries with similar sectoral profiles may have more to learn from one another than countries with similar headline emissions. Clustering makes that visible and gives climate cooperation a more practical basis: shared technology needs, finance constraints and transition challenges.

## The decision lens

The article's central question is not who is to blame. It is where action has leverage. Clean power, electrification, industrial change and targeted support need to respond to the systems that actually produce emissions.
