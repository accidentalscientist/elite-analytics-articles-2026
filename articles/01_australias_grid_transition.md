## The transition is not one line on one chart

Australia's electricity transition is often reduced to a single question: how quickly are renewables replacing coal? The NEM data tells a richer story. It tracks generation, emissions and market value across technologies over more than two decades, showing a system that is changing in volume, composition and operating rhythm at once.

Coal remains large because power systems inherit history. It was built around dispatchable thermal generation, concentrated infrastructure and predictable demand. Yet the long-run series shows that this dominance is no longer the whole story. Wind has recorded the clearest sustained growth among the established technologies; utility and rooftop solar have moved from marginal to consequential; and coal's earlier peak has given way to a gradual decline.

The practical implication is that transition planning cannot stop at annual renewable shares. A system with more variable generation also needs to manage *when* energy arrives. Solar's summer profile, wind's stronger winter and spring contribution, and hydro's seasonal behaviour point to a portfolio effect: the technologies do not move together. That is helpful, but it does not erase the need for storage, flexible demand, firming and transmission.

## Carbon intensity adds the decision lens

The second part of the analysis brings emissions intensity and market value into view. This is where the argument becomes more useful than a simple fuel leaderboard. Technologies should not only be compared by how much electricity they produce, but by the emissions attached to each unit and by the role they play in the market.

Coal's high carbon intensity makes its long-run position increasingly difficult to reconcile with a decarbonising economy. Gas can play a flexible role, but it is not a zero-emissions substitute. Wind, solar, hydro and storage therefore need to be understood as parts of a changing system, rather than as isolated competitors.

## What to watch next

Three questions follow from this evidence. First, how quickly can new transmission connect the strongest renewable resources to demand? Second, how much storage and flexible demand is needed to smooth daily and seasonal patterns? Third, how will market value evolve as low-marginal-cost generation becomes more common?

Those are not peripheral implementation details. They are the work of the transition.
