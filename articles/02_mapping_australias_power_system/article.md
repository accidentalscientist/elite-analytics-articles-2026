Australia's energy transition is often discussed as a national target. The map tells a different story: power stations, fuel types, demand centres and renewable resources are distributed unevenly across the country.

## National goals meet regional infrastructure

Generation has historically clustered along population and industrial corridors, particularly on the east coast. Those clusters reflect earlier investment choices, fuel resources and the geography of demand. They also determine the starting conditions for transition. A region with retiring coal capacity faces a different challenge from a region with strong wind or solar resources but limited transmission.

The useful question is not simply where renewable generation is located. It is where a new connection, storage asset or planning decision can unlock a more reliable, lower-emissions system. Maps make those constraints visible.

## A system is more than its average fuel mix

State and national fuel-mix comparisons show the balance between legacy thermal generation and the growing contribution of renewables. A national average can conceal sharp differences: some regions rely heavily on a legacy fleet; others have hydro, wind or solar advantages. This is why a transition policy must connect national ambition with regional sequencing, workforce planning and transmission investment.

## What this means next

The next generation of energy analysis should pair asset maps with network capacity, retirement schedules, renewable zones and demand forecasts. That would turn a static map into a decision tool: where should the system build next, and what local conditions must be solved first?
