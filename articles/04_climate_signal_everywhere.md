## Three records, one story

Climate evidence can feel abstract when it is presented as a single global average. It becomes harder to dismiss when independent measurement systems tell the same story. Long-run surface temperature anomalies show sustained warming across more than a century. Satellite observations confirm the recent trend from a different vantage point. Atmospheric carbon-dioxide records show the forcing signal that helps explain why the trend persists.

Each dataset has its own strengths and limits. Surface records provide the longest history. Satellite data offers independent recent-period coverage and detailed seasonal signals. Baseline atmospheric observations show the continuing accumulation of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere. Together, they provide a stronger narrative than any one series alone.

## The shape of the trend matters

The most important feature is not merely that recent years are warmer than earlier years. The distribution has shifted. What once counted as unusual warmth has become increasingly common. Non-linear trend modelling in the long-run record suggests acceleration, while the satellite series identifies recent extreme years and positive anomalies across seasons.

This matters because a warming climate does not behave like a gentle, uniform offset. It shifts the conditions within which infrastructure, ecosystems, cities and energy systems operate. Heat, rainfall, demand peaks and fire weather are all experienced locally, even when the underlying signal is global.

## Evidence is a starting point for action

The point of assembling these records is not to rehearse a debate that the data has already settled. It is to clarify the scale and persistence of the problem energy policy must address. A system designed around stable historical conditions will become less reliable as those conditions move.
