## Consequence and cause belong in the same frame

Climate reporting often separates impacts from emissions. One story describes rising seas; another lists the sectors producing greenhouse gases. The separation is understandable, but it weakens the connection between evidence and action.

This analysis brings the two together. Long-run sea-level observations provide a measurable record of climate impact. Sectoral emissions data reveals the sources that have the most leverage over the future trajectory. The pairing does not claim that one month of emissions causes one month of sea-level change. Instead, it connects the physical consequence to the systems that must change.

## The signal is already visible

The sea-level record shows a persistent upward trend through time, with shorter-term variation sitting on top of a clear long-run direction. For coastal communities, the significance is not confined to a distant projection. A higher baseline amplifies the damage from storm surge, king tides and other coastal extremes.

## Prioritisation is the practical challenge

Sectoral emissions are not evenly distributed. That means decarbonisation cannot be a scatter of small symbolic actions. The biggest gains come from targeting the parts of the economy where technologies, infrastructure and policy can change large emission flows: electricity, transport, industry, buildings and land use.

The transition is difficult precisely because these sectors are connected. Clean electricity enables lower-emissions transport and heating. Transmission and storage determine how much variable renewable generation can be absorbed. Industrial policy affects materials and supply chains. Good analysis helps locate the leverage points without pretending there is a single switch to pull.
