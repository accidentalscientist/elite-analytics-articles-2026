## The headline number is not the whole profile

Global emissions debate is usually organised around totals. Totals matter: the largest emitters have an outsized influence on the global trajectory. But a total alone does not show *how* a country emits, what infrastructure it relies on, or which transition challenge it faces.

Country and sector data make those distinctions visible. Some economies are dominated by power-sector emissions. Others have heavy industrial, transport, residential or land-use components. Similar total emissions can mask very different systems.

## From comparison to clustering

The analysis first compares emissions over time and across major economies. It then uses clustering to group countries with similar sectoral profiles. This is valuable because it moves the discussion away from a simplistic divide between “leaders” and “laggards”. Countries in the same cluster may face similar technology needs, finance constraints or policy trade-offs even if their income and population differ.

The cluster view also makes international collaboration more concrete. A country accelerating clean power may have more to learn from another country with a similar generation profile than from a country that happens to have the same total emissions.

## What fair policy looks like in practice

Fairness is often described as a question of how much each country should cut. It is also a question of how countries can cut. Technology transfer, concessional finance, grid planning, industrial partnerships and shared standards all work better when they respond to real emission structures.

The implication is not that data removes politics. It does something more useful: it makes the trade-offs explicit. Better comparisons produce better questions, and better questions are the beginning of durable cooperation.
