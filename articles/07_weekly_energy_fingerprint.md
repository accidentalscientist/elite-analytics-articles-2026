## Demand has a daily rhythm

Electricity systems are usually discussed through supply: coal closures, wind farms, solar installations and batteries. Demand is the other half of the equation. It is shaped by when people wake, work, cook, travel and rest—and those routines leave a remarkably clear signature in household power data.

The weekday-versus-weekend comparison shows that timing shifts with daily life. Weekday use follows the structure of work and school hours; weekend activity moves later and spreads differently through the day. The heatmap turns that pattern into a weekly fingerprint.

## Why this matters for a renewable grid

A grid with more wind and solar has more low-cost energy at particular times. If demand can respond—through smart appliances, electric-vehicle charging, storage, flexible industry or well-designed tariffs—the system can use more of that clean supply and rely less on expensive firming.

That does not mean households should be treated as a problem to manage. It means they can be participants in a better system. Flexible demand works best when it is simple, voluntary and beneficial: consumers receive clear value, while the grid gains room to operate.

## Small data, useful insight

This is a modest dataset, but it demonstrates a larger point. Fine-grained energy data can reveal operational patterns that annual totals hide. It can help identify when efficiency measures matter most, where demand-response programs could work, and how people might experience the energy transition in everyday life.
