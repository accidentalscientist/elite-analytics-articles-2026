National inequality figures are useful, but they can obscure the cities where economic difference is actually lived. Metropolitan data offers a closer view of how prosperity is shared—or not shared—within countries.

## Coverage is part of the evidence

Before comparing cities, the analysis asks where data is complete enough to support comparison. That is not a technical footnote. Data gaps determine which places can be monitored and which changes become visible to policy makers.

## Why city-level trends matter

Countries can show stable national inequality while their major cities move in different directions. Tracking metropolitan patterns through time helps distinguish broad national change from local divergence. It is especially relevant for housing, transport, employment and energy policy, all of which land in unequal places.
